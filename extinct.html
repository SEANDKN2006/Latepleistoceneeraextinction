The Pleistocene Megafauna

The Late Pleistocene witnessed the extinction of many large animals (megafauna) across most continents. These extinctions occurred particularly toward the end of the epoch, between 50,000 and 10,000 years ago. The causes remain debated but likely involved climate change and human hunting pressure.

Woolly mammoths were among the most iconic megafauna

Saber-toothed cats were formidable predators

Notable Extinct Species

Woolly Mammoth (Mammuthus primigenius)
Eurasia North America

Adapted to cold with long fur and fat layers. Survived on Wrangel Island until about 4,000 years ago.

Saber-toothed Cat (Smilodon fatalis)
North America South America

Powerful predators with elongated canine teeth. Hunted large herbivores like bison and camels.

Giant Ground Sloth (Megatherium americanum)
South America

Massive herbivores reaching sizes of elephants. Some species had bony armor.

Woolly Rhinoceros (Coelodonta antiquitatis)
Eurasia

Cold-adapted rhinos with thick fur and a flat horn for sweeping snow.

Irish Elk (Megaloceros giganteus)
Eurasia

Giant deer with the largest antlers of any known deer species (up to 3.65 m wide).

Diprotodon
Australia

Largest known marsupial, about the size of a hippopotamus.

Short-faced Bear (Arctodus simus)
North America

One of the largest known terrestrial mammalian carnivores, standing up to 4.6 m tall when upright.

Extinction Patterns

The Late Pleistocene extinctions were most severe in the Americas and Australia, where about 80% of large mammal genera disappeared. Africa and southern Asia were less affected, possibly because species there had co-evolved with humans.
